Mapping How Chinese Immigrants Rename New York
Immigrants make up almost 60% of the population in Manhattan’s Chinatown, and many of them do not speak English. This map explores what New York sounds like to them.  Click the names of different streets, neighborhoods and landmarks to hear how Chinese immigrants in Lower Manhattan have renamed the city's streets, parks, and other landmarks in their respective dialects. The process of renaming, which probably begins out of convenience and expediency, is one way that immigrants can co-opt their sections of the city, making them more legible and familiar to their community. It's a parallel New York City you can spend your whole life in without ever really knowing.

One Street, Three Dialects

China’s dialects are nearly as numerous as the country’s towns and cities. Similarly, several dialects can be heard in the streets and shops of Manhattan’s Chinatown. In this way, every street can have a dozen pronunciations, and thus, names.

Where Do The Chinese Names Come From?

Some are named by taking the literal translations of the word.

For example, many Mandarin Speakers refer to “Market Street” as Shichang Jie, or 市场街, which is literally “market street.”

Alternatively, some streets are named by using phonemes from Chinese to sound out the street’s English names.

For example, Ludlow street in Cantonese is pronounced “Lut Low Gai” (“gai” is Cantonese for “street”)
